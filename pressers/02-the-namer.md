## **John McCarthy: The Branding of an Era (1955)**

### **The Human**

It’s 1955. John McCarthy is a young Assistant Professor of Mathematics at Dartmouth. He is brilliant, a bit restless, and convinced that "thinking" isn't just for humans. But he has a practical problem: he needs to convince the Rockefeller Foundation to fund a two-month summer research project for himself and a few friends.

### **The Button**

In the formal proposal for the "Dartmouth Summer Research Project," McCarthy had to describe what they were actually going to study. He could have called it "Complex Information Processing" or "Automated Logic." Instead, he chose a provocative, slightly sci-fi label that he thought would grab attention: **"Artificial Intelligence."**

He "pressed the button" on a name that even his colleagues, like Herb Simon and Allen Newell, originally disliked because they thought it sounded too flashy and unscientific.

### **The Ripple**

McCarthy didn’t just get the funding; he gave a fragmented field a **unified identity**.

* **The Industry:** By choosing those two words, he created a "North Star" that billions of dollars in investment would eventually follow.
* **The Public Imagination:** The name "Artificial Intelligence" is responsible for the way we talk about machines today—as entities with "intelligence" rather than just "calculators."
* **The 70-Year Career:** We are still living in the house McCarthy built. Every time you see "AI" in a headline, you are seeing the result of a young professor's marketing choice in a 1955 grant application.
