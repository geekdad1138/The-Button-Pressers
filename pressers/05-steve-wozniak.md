### **Steve Wozniak: The Eureka Moment at Homebrew (1975)**

#### **The Human**

It’s March 5, 1975. Steve Wozniak is a 24-year-old engineer at HP. He is painfully shy—the kind of guy who "never once raised his hand to speak" in a meeting. He’s been dragged to a rainy garage in Menlo Park by a friend to check out the first meeting of the **Homebrew Computer Club**. He feels out of place, surrounded by hobbyists talking about the Altair 8800, a machine he didn't even know existed.

#### **The Button**

As a data sheet for the new 8008 microprocessor is passed around the room, Wozniak has a "Eureka" moment. He realizes that the chips have finally become powerful enough to do what he’d been dreaming of since high school. That night, he goes home and "presses the button" on a design: he decides to take a microprocessor and hook it up to a TV and a keyboard—something no one else was doing. He just wanted to "blow the club members away" with how simple and affordable a computer could be.

#### **The Ripple**

Wozniak famously handed out his designs for free at the club, but his friend Steve Jobs saw something else.

* **The Personal Computer:** That "simple" design became the **Apple I**. It proved that a computer didn't need to be a giant cabinet; it could be a tool for a person.
* **The Blueprint:** Every laptop, tablet, and smartphone on earth follows the logic Wozniak laid out that night: a central processor connected to a screen and a keyboard. He pressed the button on the **Democratization of Computing** just because he wanted to impress his fellow geeks.
