# The Architect

## **Margaret Hamilton: The Architect of "Error Recovery" (1969)**

### **The Human**

It’s 1969. Margaret is a young working mother and the lead software engineer for the Apollo 11 moon landing. At the time, "software" wasn't even a respected term—it was seen as a secondary task to the "real" hardware engineering. She used to bring her young daughter to the lab at MIT while she hand-coded the flight software.

### **The Button**

During a simulation, her daughter accidentally crashed the system by pressing a button she shouldn't have. Instead of just laughing it off, Margaret "pressed the button" on **rigorous foresight**. She insisted on adding "error recovery" code to the Apollo computer—code that would allow the machine to prioritize important tasks (like landing) over unimportant ones if the system got overloaded. Her bosses told her it wasn't necessary because "astronauts are trained never to make mistakes."

### **The Ripple**

On July 20, 1969, as the Eagle was descending to the lunar surface, a radar switch was accidentally left in the wrong position, flooding the computer with data. The alarms went off. Because of Margaret’s "unnecessary" code, the computer didn't crash; it simply wiped the low-priority tasks and focused on the landing.

* **The Software Engineer:** She coined the term "Software Engineering" to demand respect for the field.
* **The Mission Saver:** Without her specific decision to plan for human error, Apollo 11 likely would have aborted—or crashed.
